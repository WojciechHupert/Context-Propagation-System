How Does It Work?

A continuity system for turning moments into social memory.

CPS turns a moment into social memory, then tracks what that memory does next.

A conversation, event, or observation enters the system. CPS extracts what matters, stores it as structured memory, and decides who can know it.

When information spreads, it does not move as a perfect copy. It carries source, uncertainty, emotional weight, containment rules, and the possibility of distortion.

After information enters a group, CPS asks four practical questions.

Question 01

Who knows?

Memory is attached to agents, roles, privacy boundaries, and sources instead of being treated as a single global fact.

Question 02

Can it move?

A secret may stay private. A rumor may travel. A warning may spread unevenly through trust, fear, or status.

Question 03

How does it change?

Information can lose certainty, gain emotional charge, become distorted, or return later with a different tone.

Question 04

What does it cause?

The important output is continuity: later reactions that make sense because the world remembers what happened.

From moment to consequence.

CPS is easiest to read as a chain: something happens, the system gives it shape, Studio keeps it visible, and the world reacts later.

Foundations

CPS is currently a bounded prototype built around local-first interaction, structured memory, traceable propagation, durable persistence, and CPS Studio observability. The system is designed to separate interpretation from state. Language models may help interpret conversations and generate responses, but CPS preserves the underlying structure: source, ownership, confidence, privacy, lineage, memory records, and history. That makes the project more than a conversation interface. It is an exploratory infrastructure for social memory in artificial worlds, built to make propagation, containment, and consequence visible and inspectable.
